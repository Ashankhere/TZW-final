Wood Sponges: Recovering Waste Oil



Today, every section of society uses oil in one way or another. From domestic to industrial use, from automobiles to water-borne vessels, oil is a non-avoidable asset. Every by-product of crude oil is used in different aspects. We found oil in the pores of the rock deep beneath the surface. Oil gets generated in the source rock and is contained in the reservoir rock. Seal or cap rock acts as a barrier to further migrate this hydrocarbon fluid.


The petroleum system



Oil spills have been every day since exploration times. It is a general term in which liquid petroleum hydrocarbon leaks into the water and on land. Leakage occurs on the drilling site due to breakage of the pipeline, sinking of oil tankers, drilling operations & naturally, when cap rock is permeable, and the hydrocarbon present in the source rock rises to the surface. During an oil spill, hundreds of thousands of metric tons of oil are wasted, affecting aquatic life, marine mammals and plants, animals, and birds. The Niger Delta of Nigeria is an example of when an oil spilled area catches fire; it costs us economically and ecologically.

Since oil spills cause a significant threat to living organisms, scientists are working on how to recover the waste oil. Some of the techniques which cause secondary pollution are pretty cheap but non-efficient. Offshore removal can be done by skimming the oil spill, Floating booms, absorbing oil from the water surface by volcanic ash, burning of oil, and bioremediation. Onshore removal is laborious as oil seeps into sandy beaches, which is challenging to extract. Nowadays, a new, more efficient technique is being developed by scientists around the world. They created a Wood Sponge, which selectively absorbs oil and is reusable. This sponge is constructed from wood and shows excellent tolerance to repeated cycling of absorption and squeezing. Other similar researches are either made up of non-renewable sources or are not very efficient. It absorbs approximately 40 times its weight and ten cycles of absorption when tested with silicone oil in water.


As shown in the figure, The sponge has no affinity for
water and absorbs oil completely.

This sponge is made from a chemically-modified version of nanofibrillated cellulose (NFC) or nanocellulose. It is made to lose its hydrophilic properties to only absorb oil. In lab tests, it effectively absorbs engine oil, silicone oil, ethanol, acetone, chloroform from water samples, remains floating and is biodegradable.

This work is now being scaled for oil spill-size. With this new technology, not only the oil companies will be able to recover much oil, but proper implementation can save aquatic life too. Since there will be no burning of oil spills, no harmful gases will be released into the atmosphere. Using this sponge will help the companies economically while simultaneously supporting the environment.
